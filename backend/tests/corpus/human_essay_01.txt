I got my first dog when I was twelve. His name was Chester, a mutt we picked up from the shelter on Route 9. He had one ear that stuck up and one that flopped over, and he smelled terrible no matter how many baths we gave him.

My mom didn't want a dog. She said they were too much work, which was probably true. But my dad had grown up with dogs his whole life and he wore her down over the course of about six months. Every Sunday at dinner he'd bring it up again. "The kids need a dog," he'd say, and my mom would roll her eyes.

Chester turned out to be a terrible dog in a lot of ways. He chewed through two pairs of my dad's work boots. He barked at the mailman so aggressively that we got a letter from the post office. Once he ate an entire stick of butter off the counter and threw up on the living room rug.

But he was also the best dog. He'd sit with me on the porch steps after school and lean his head against my knee. When I was sick he'd sleep on my bed, which he wasn't supposed to do, but my mom pretended not to notice. He knew when you were sad. Dogs just know.

Chester lived to be fourteen, which the vet said was pretty good for his size. When he died I was in college and I drove three hours home in the middle of the week. We buried him in the backyard near the fence where he used to dig holes.

I have my own dog now. She's a beagle named Rosie. She doesn't chew shoes but she howls at sirens. My kids love her. Sometimes when she leans against my leg on the porch I think about Chester and Route 9 and how my dad was right about the kids needing a dog.
