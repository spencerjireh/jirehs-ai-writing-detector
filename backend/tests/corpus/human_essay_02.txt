The summer I turned sixteen I worked at a gas station off the interstate. It was the kind of place truckers stopped at because it had showers in the back and decent coffee. My uncle owned it and he paid me cash, seven dollars an hour, which seemed like a fortune at the time.

Most of my shifts were the early morning ones, five to noon. I'd get there while it was still dark and unlock the pumps and start the coffee machines. There were three coffee machines and they all leaked. I must have gone through a roll of paper towels every morning just wiping up coffee drips.

The regulars were mostly the same guys every day. There was a plumber named Gus who got a large black coffee and two packs of Marlboros. He never said much. There was a woman named Deb who drove a school bus and always bought a banana and a Pepsi, which I thought was a weird breakfast but I never said anything.

The worst part of the job was cleaning the bathrooms. The best part was when it was slow and I could sit behind the counter and read. I got through most of Kurt Vonnegut that summer. Slaughterhouse-Five, Cat's Cradle, Breakfast of Champions. My uncle didn't care as long as I looked up when the bell rang.

I learned some things that summer that I didn't expect. I learned that most people are polite if you're polite to them first. I learned that standing on concrete for seven hours makes your back hurt in a specific way that sitting never does. I learned that I did not want to work at a gas station for the rest of my life, which I guess was the point.

My uncle sold the place a few years later. Last time I drove past it was a tire shop. The coffee machines are probably gone. But sometimes when I smell that particular burned-coffee smell I'm right back behind that counter, sixteen years old, reading Vonnegut and waiting for the bell.
